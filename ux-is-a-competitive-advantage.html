UX is a Competitive Advantage

- May 26, 2014-

I’ve been thinking a lot lately about what makes a good product, and how it relates to creating a good business. Recently, I read the article Design doesn’t deserve a seat at the table. It is an interesting article about who should be involved in the direction that leads to a successful business, but something just didn’t sit right with me.

For all I know it was written specifically to be controversial. But I’ll bite, in case people start to believe the article. Ironically, it is written by a product designer. I’ve dabbled in product management, but I am most certainly an engineer by trade. I want to defend why design does deserve a seat at the table.

The article speaks to two types of value. Real value and perceived value. The claim is that design can multiply real value, and create perceived value. However, it cannot create real value, and a business cannot survive without real value. One key point he mentions during that section of the article is:

Design can multiply utility that already exists, as it certainly did with the iPhone, but design isn’t the hero.

Therefore designers should not be given the power to be a part of business decisions. I believe there are some fundamental disconnects that could lead to this conclusion. So I’ll look at these first, before addressing the individual arguments in the article.

What qualifies as a designer?

All to often, design and UI are conflated. UI design is a part of design. It is not design’s sole purpose. In software, the main areas of design are UI design, interaction design, and experience design. In order to avoid a detailed definition of each one, let’s approximate the difference as beauty vs utility. UI design and experience design contribute to beauty, and interaction design and experience design contribute to utility. If I were to hire someone as a designer they would need all three skillsets. An artist, therefore is not a designer.

Technology and Design: The chicken and egg

Imagine the invention of an algorithm that can find the answer to the human existence, but on current hardware it will take one trillion years to finish. If we wait one thousand years hardware will be powerful enough to finish the computations in only one million years. For the next one thousand years the technology to find the answer to the human existence is essentially useless. Now image a beautiful painting. Let’s say Monet’s Water Lilies.

Technology can exist without design. Design can exist without technology. I propose that a successful business absolutely cannot with no exceptions, exist without design. Let’s assume that a business cannot exist without real value. Real value is defined as solving a problem for which a consumer will give money or time in return. If there is a problem to be solved then the technology used to solve the problem must have utility. Technology does not innately have utility. Utility must be applied to technology via design. Therefore, real value cannot exist without design.

Unless a consumer is willing to pay for a technology they cannot use, there is some interaction design inherent to the product. You might argue, “Well okay, whatever. You didn’t need to get all technical. Some products can be successful with minimal design. Anyone can do it, so it isn’t important.”.

However, the point here is that design is the egg. Design gives technology value. As an engineer I am all too familiar with the sentiment of “Wow that technology is really cool, but how do I use it?”. A business without good design leaves itself open to displacement by a company that uses the same technology, but delivers greater utility.

Rebuttal

  • Design cannot create real value: A key component of value that people often pay for, that I pay for is the saving of time. Products that are easier to use, and save people time can displace clunkier competitors without new technology. Technology is in play, but people don’t want technology. They want solutions. They want utility to be applied to technology so it can solve their problems. In order to refute this answer you would have to claim that it is impossible to save people time using interaction design. And claim that only technology can save people time.
  • Design can multiply utility that already exists, but it is not the hero: Design is what gives utility to technology. Design can certainly use technology as a means to an end. Ask yourself why the screen technology used in the iPhone would contribute value. The only reason I can think of is that it gives the user a better experience. So then, do the engineers choose to use this technology because of some random reason that coincides with user experience? Or do they choose it because they’re told, and are even a part of trying to create the best possible product for their users?

Many other claims in the article can also be traced back to design. Starbucks location, quality, and service? Sounds like something that affects the user experience.

Conclusion

I am not claiming that design is the only useful component of a successful business. Likewise, neither is the article at the top of this one trying to claim that technology is the only useful component. It seems like sometimes we see that other components contribute heavily to the success of a particular company, whether that be technology, marketing, sales, or customer support. We also see that design is usually not involved in the high-level decisions in a company. However, it is design that drives the utility of a product.

If you believe in execution, design is a part of execution. If you believe people narrowly focused on design should not have C-level involvement that’s fine. An engineer who does not care about the other aspects of the business would probably be treated the same way. I believe that it is dangerous to actively preach that design should not be involved in business decisions. You just might end up with a product that no one can use, waiting until someone makes a product that someone can use.
